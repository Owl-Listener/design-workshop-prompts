Stakeholder Interviews

Prepare smarter questions, synthesize what you heard, and surface the real priorities hiding underneath the stated ones.

Generate a tailored interview script
Facilitator
I'm interviewing [role/name] about [project or initiative]. They care most about [known priorities]. I have 30 minutes. Generate an interview script with: - 2 warm-up questions to build rapport - 5 core questions that surface their real concerns (not just stated priorities) - 2 questions that test assumptions we've been making - 1 closing question that opens the door to things I haven't thought to ask For each question, include a brief note on what I'm actually listening for in their answer.
Tip: Replace the bracketed sections with your specifics. The more context you give, the sharper the questions.
Debrief and extract themes from a session
Facilitator
Here are my raw notes from a stakeholder interview: [Paste your notes] Analyze this and give me: 1. The 3 strongest themes across their answers 2. Any contradictions between what they said they want and what they actually seem to need 3. Assumptions they're making that we should test 4. A one-paragraph summary I could share with my team 5. Two follow-up questions I should ask next time
Prepare your own talking points
Participant
I'm a [your role] about to be interviewed by the design team about [project]. I want to communicate clearly and make sure my perspective is actually useful. Help me prepare: - 3 things I should definitely mention (based on what design teams typically need to hear from someone in my role) - 2 things I should be honest about even if they're uncomfortable - A way to frame my constraints without sounding like I'm blocking progress
Compare perspectives across multiple interviews
Facilitator
I've interviewed these stakeholders about [project]: [Paste summaries or key quotes from each person] Compare their perspectives and show me: 1. Where they genuinely agree (shared ground I can build on) 2. Where they think they agree but actually mean different things 3. Where they openly disagree and what's driving the disagreement 4. The single biggest risk if I design without resolving these tensions
Turn interview insights into design principles
Both
Based on these stakeholder interview themes: [Paste your themes or summary] Draft 4-6 design principles that: - Are specific enough to actually guide decisions (not generic "be user-friendly") - Each one resolves a real tension that came up in the interviews - Include a "this, not that" example for each one - Could be printed on a wall and used during design reviews

Design Sprints

Prompts for every phase of a sprint, from challenge framing on Monday to prototype testing on Friday. Keep momentum when the room stalls.

Frame the sprint challenge
Facilitator
We're running a design sprint on [topic/problem]. Here's what we know: - Business context: [brief description] - Users affected: [who] - Current pain points: [what's broken or missing] - Constraints: [time, tech, budget, politics] Help me write a sprint challenge question in the format: "How might we [outcome] for [user] so that [impact]?" Give me 3 versions: one ambitious, one pragmatic, one that reframes the problem entirely. For each, explain what it would focus the team on and what it would leave out.
Generate "How Might We" notes
Participant
Our sprint challenge is: "[paste the HMW question]" I need to generate How Might We notes during our sprint. Help me think divergently by generating 15 HMW questions across these angles: - Reframe the problem (what if the opposite were true?) - Amplify what's already working - Remove a constraint entirely - Serve an extreme user - Break the problem into smaller parts Make each one specific enough to spark a real idea, not so broad it could mean anything.
Sketch session: generate solution concepts
Participant
I'm in a design sprint sketch session. Our challenge is: "[HMW question]" Help me brainstorm 6 distinct solution directions. For each one, give me: - A descriptive name (2-3 words) - The core idea in one sentence - What makes this approach different from the others - The biggest assumption it relies on I'm looking for range, not refinement. Push me past the obvious solutions.
Write a test script for prototype Friday
Facilitator
We've built a prototype for [brief description]. We're testing with [number] users on Friday. Our top 3 questions to answer: 1. [question] 2. [question] 3. [question] Write a 30-minute usability test script with: - An intro that puts participants at ease and sets expectations - 4-5 task scenarios that test our questions (phrased as realistic goals, not instructions) - Probing follow-up questions for each task - A debrief section to capture reactions - A note-taking template I can hand to observers
Unstick a stalled sprint room
Facilitator
My sprint team is stuck. We're on [day/phase] and the energy has dropped because [describe what happened: can't agree, too many ideas, solution feels obvious/boring, scope creep, etc.]. The room has [number] people: [roles]. Give me: 1. A diagnosis of what's likely going wrong (based on common sprint dynamics) 2. A 10-minute exercise I can run right now to shift the energy 3. A reframe of the problem that might unlock new thinking 4. The one question I should ask the room to get things moving again
Sprint retrospective synthesis
Both
We just finished a design sprint. Here's what happened: [Paste: what we built, what we tested, key findings, team reactions] Help me write a sprint retrospective that covers: 1. What we learned (the actual insights, not just "users liked it") 2. What surprised us 3. What we'd do differently next sprint 4. The clearest next step coming out of this 5. A one-paragraph "elevator pitch" summary I can send to leadership

Critique Sessions

Give and receive better feedback. Structure critiques so they surface what actually matters, not just what's easiest to comment on.

Set up a critique with clear framing
Facilitator
I'm presenting [design/concept] at a critique session. The audience is [who]. The work is at [stage: early exploration / mid-fidelity / near-final]. Write me a critique setup brief that includes: - A 2-sentence framing of what this design is trying to do - 3 specific questions I want feedback on (calibrated to the stage of the work) - What kind of feedback is helpful right now and what isn't - A suggested format for the session (silent review + discussion, round-robin, etc.) - A time structure for a [30/45/60]-minute session
Generate structured feedback on a design
Participant
I need to give critique feedback on this design: [describe or paste screenshot description] The designer asked for feedback on: [their specific questions] Help me structure my feedback using this framework: 1. What's working well and why (be specific, not just "looks good") 2. What's confusing or creating friction, and for whom 3. Questions I'd want to answer before going further 4. One alternative approach worth exploring, with a clear rationale Keep the tone constructive. I want to push the work forward, not just react to it.
Synthesize critique notes into action items
Facilitator
Here are the raw notes from our design critique: [Paste all feedback] Synthesize this into: 1. Feedback themes (group similar comments together) 2. Conflicts (where reviewers disagree, and what's driving the disagreement) 3. A prioritized action list: what to change now, what to explore, what to park 4. Any feedback that's actually a requirements question (and who to ask) 5. One thing multiple people praised that I should protect as I iterate
Challenge your own design before critique
Participant
I'm about to put this design into critique: [describe your design and its intent] Play the role of 4 different critics, each with a distinct lens: 1. The user advocate (is this actually solving a real problem?) 2. The systems thinker (how does this affect the broader product?) 3. The accessibility reviewer (who might this exclude?) 4. The skeptic (what's the weakest assumption here?) For each perspective, give me the single toughest question they'd ask. Then tell me which one I should have the best answer for before I walk into the room.
Facilitate a "silent critique" exercise
Facilitator
I want to run a silent critique where reviewers write feedback independently before discussing. We have [number] reviewers and [number] designs to review. Design the exercise for me: 1. A brief explanation I can read to the group 2. The exact prompts/questions each reviewer should answer in writing (keep to 3-4) 3. How long to give for silent review vs. discussion 4. How to facilitate the discussion so quieter voices get heard 5. A template I can paste into a shared doc for reviewers to fill in
Turn vague feedback into specific direction
Both
I received this feedback on my design: "[paste the vague or confusing feedback]" Help me decode it: 1. What they're probably actually saying (translate from vague to specific) 2. What design element or decision is most likely triggering this reaction 3. Two concrete things I could try in response 4. A clarifying question I could ask them to confirm I'm reading it right

Research Synthesis

Turn messy research data into patterns, insights, and stories that move rooms. The hardest part of research isn't collecting, it's making sense.

Run an affinity mapping exercise
Facilitator
I have [number] research observations from [interviews/usability tests/surveys]: [Paste your observations, one per line] Help me run an affinity mapping exercise: 1. Group these into clusters based on underlying themes (not surface similarity) 2. Name each cluster with an insight statement, not a topic label (e.g., "Users create workarounds because the main flow assumes expertise" not "Workarounds") 3. Identify which clusters have the most observations (signal strength) 4. Flag any outliers that don't fit a cluster but feel important 5. Suggest which 3 clusters the team should focus on first and why
Write insight statements from raw findings
Both
Here are my raw research findings: [Paste findings] Turn these into insight statements using this structure: "[User type] [behavior or belief] because [underlying motivation or context], which means [implication for design]." For each insight: - Rate the evidence strength (strong / moderate / emerging) - Note what would make the evidence stronger - Suggest one design implication Give me 5-8 insight statements, prioritized by strength of evidence.
Build a journey map from research data
Facilitator
Based on this research about [user type] doing [task/process]: [Paste your research data: quotes, observations, survey responses] Build a journey map that includes: - 5-7 phases of their experience - What they're doing, thinking, and feeling at each phase - Pain points and bright spots - The emotional arc (where does frustration peak? where does confidence grow?) - Opportunities for design intervention at each phase Format it as a table I can paste into a collaborative doc or whiteboard.
Find what you're missing in your research
Participant
I've been synthesizing research on [topic]. Here's what I've found so far: [Paste your current themes/insights] My research included: [methods used, number of participants, who they were] Help me identify: 1. Perspectives I might be missing (whose voice isn't represented?) 2. Questions my current insights don't answer 3. Potential biases in my sample or methods 4. Where I'm making a leap from data to conclusion that needs more evidence 5. One provocative "what if the opposite were true?" challenge to my strongest finding
Write a research readout that moves people
Facilitator
I need to present research findings to [audience: leadership / product team / engineering / cross-functional]. They have [15/30/45] minutes and care most about [what they want to know]. My key findings are: [Paste your findings] Write me a presentation outline that: - Opens with the single most compelling finding (not methodology) - Uses participant quotes strategically (max 3-4) - Builds to a clear "so what" moment - Ends with specific, actionable recommendations (not just "more research needed") - Includes talking points for each section The goal is for people to leave the room ready to act, not just informed.
Create a persona from research data
Both
Based on this research data: [Paste interview excerpts, survey data, behavioral observations] Create a research-backed persona that includes: - A name and realistic context (not a stereotype) - Their primary goal and what's blocking them - Behaviors I actually observed (not assumed) - A direct quote that captures their mindset - The key tension in their experience (what they want vs. what they have to do) - How they differ from the other user types we've identified: [list them] Make this persona feel like a real person, not a marketing archetype.

Prioritization Exercises

When everything feels urgent, these prompts help your team make real choices, not just rearrange the deck chairs.

Facilitate an impact-effort prioritization
Facilitator
We need to prioritize these design opportunities: [List your opportunities/features/ideas] For each one, help me assess: - User impact (how much does this improve someone's experience? low / medium / high) - Business impact (does this move a metric leadership cares about? low / medium / high) - Effort (design + engineering + testing, roughly: small / medium / large) - Risk (what could go wrong and how bad would it be?) Then place them in a 2x2 matrix (high impact + low effort = do first) and explain your reasoning. Flag any where the "obvious" quadrant might be misleading.
"Kill your darlings" exercise
Both
We have too many features/ideas on the table and need to cut scope. Here's what we're considering: [List everything on the table] Our constraints: [timeline, team size, dependencies] Our core user need: [the one thing we must solve] Force-rank these by asking: 1. If we could only ship ONE thing, which would it be? Why? 2. For everything else: what happens to the user if we don't build this? (If the answer is "not much," cut it) 3. Which items are we keeping because we're attached, not because users need them? 4. What's the minimum viable version that actually solves the core need? Be honest with me. I need someone to help me let go.
Run a "MoSCoW" prioritization workshop
Facilitator
I'm facilitating a MoSCoW prioritization session with [team/stakeholders]. We're prioritizing [what] for [timeline]. Here are all items to prioritize: [List them] Help me: 1. Write a 2-minute explanation of MoSCoW I can read to the group (Must have / Should have / Could have / Won't have this time) 2. Pre-sort these items with a rationale for each, so I have a starting position to react to 3. Identify the items that will cause the most debate and prepare a question for each that helps resolve it 4. Set a rule for the maximum percentage of effort that can be "Must have" (typically 60%) 5. Write a summary template I can fill in after the session
Build a case for your priority
Participant
I need to argue that [feature/initiative] should be prioritized higher. My audience is [who] and they care about [their priorities]. Help me build a 2-minute pitch that: - Opens with the user problem (make them feel it) - Connects to a metric or outcome they already care about - Addresses the most likely objection ("but what about [competing priority]?") - Proposes a scope that's small enough to say yes to - Ends with a specific ask, not a vague "we should think about this" Keep it honest. If the case is weak, tell me where to strengthen it.
Resolve a prioritization deadlock
Facilitator
Our team is deadlocked on prioritization. The debate is between: Option A: [describe] Option B: [describe] The people arguing for A care about: [their reasoning] The people arguing for B care about: [their reasoning] Help me facilitate a resolution: 1. Name the underlying values conflict (what does each side actually value?) 2. Is there a "both/and" option that addresses both concerns? 3. If we must choose: what's a fair decision framework we can agree on before applying it? 4. A time-boxed experiment we could run instead of deciding permanently 5. The single question that, if answered, would make the choice obvious
Evaluate trade-offs honestly
Both
We're considering this trade-off: [Describe the decision: e.g., "Ship a simpler version now vs. build the full feature over 3 months"] Help me think through this clearly: 1. What do we gain with each option? (Be specific, not just "speed" or "quality") 2. What do we lose with each option? (Be honest about the real costs) 3. Who is affected most by each choice and how would they feel about it? 4. What's reversible and what isn't? 5. In 6 months, which decision will we be glad we made? Don't tell me what to choose. Help me see the full picture.

Roadmap Planning

Connect design decisions to strategy. Make roadmaps that tell a story, not just list features in order.

Build a Now / Next / Later roadmap
Facilitator
I need to organize our design work into a Now / Next / Later roadmap. Here's everything on our plate: [List all initiatives, features, debt, and research] Our team: [size and composition] Our strategy: [what are we trying to achieve this quarter/half?] Key constraints: [dependencies, deadlines, unknowns] Help me: 1. Sort items into Now (this sprint/month), Next (next 1-3 months), Later (3-6 months) 2. For each bucket, explain the rationale (why now? why wait?) 3. Identify dependencies between items 4. Flag anything that's on the list but probably shouldn't be 5. Write a one-paragraph narrative that turns this list into a story ("First we'll... which enables... so that eventually...")
Stress-test your roadmap
Both
Here's our current design roadmap: [Paste your roadmap] Stress-test it by asking: 1. If our top priority takes 2x longer than expected, what breaks? 2. What happens if we lose a team member for 6 weeks? 3. Which items are we doing because of inertia vs. genuine strategic value? 4. Is there a dependency we haven't named that could block everything? 5. What would a new design director cut from this on their first day? 6. Are we solving last quarter's problem or this quarter's? Give me the honest version, not the comfortable one.
Translate design roadmap for leadership
Facilitator
I need to present our design roadmap to [VP/Director/C-level]. They care about [business outcomes, timelines, risk, team health — pick what matters most]. Here's the roadmap: [Paste it] Translate it into a leadership-ready format: 1. A 3-sentence executive summary (outcome-focused, not activity-focused) 2. Each initiative reframed as "this enables [business outcome]" not "we're designing [feature]" 3. The top 2 risks and what we're doing about them 4. What we're intentionally NOT doing and why (this builds trust) 5. One decision or resource we need from them to stay on track
Propose adding something to the roadmap
Participant
I want to propose adding [initiative/project] to our design roadmap. Here's my case: - The problem: [what's broken or missing] - Who it affects: [users, internal teams, business metrics] - Evidence: [research, data, complaints, competitive pressure] - Rough effort: [my best guess] Help me write a one-page proposal that: 1. Frames this in terms of what the team already cares about (don't introduce new goals) 2. Shows what it would replace or what else would need to shift 3. Proposes the smallest useful version (don't ask for the moon) 4. Anticipates the "why now?" question with a clear answer 5. Includes a "what if we don't" scenario that makes the cost of inaction concrete
Run a quarterly planning workshop
Facilitator
I'm facilitating a quarterly design planning workshop. The team is [size and roles]. We have [time]. Input: [what's carrying over, new requests, research insights, leadership priorities] Design a workshop agenda that: 1. Opens with a 10-minute reflection on last quarter (what we shipped, what we learned, what we didn't get to) 2. Includes an exercise to surface new opportunities the team sees 3. Has a structured way to evaluate and compare opportunities 4. Ends with clear commitments: what we'll do, what we won't, and who owns what 5. Builds in time for the "uncomfortable conversation" (the thing nobody wants to say about capacity, scope, or strategy) Include facilitation notes: how to keep it moving, what to do if it stalls, and how to handle the loudest voice in the room.
Write a roadmap narrative
Both
Here's our roadmap as a list of items: [Paste the list] Turn it into a narrative. Not a bulleted list with timeframes, but a story: - Why are we doing these things in this order? - What user experience are we building toward? - What's the "before" and "after" for our users 6 months from now? - How does each phase build on the last? Write it as 3-4 paragraphs I could put at the top of a planning doc or read at the start of a team meeting. It should make someone who just joined the team understand not just what we're doing, but why it matters.

How to get the most from these prompts

  • These work with any LLM: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or whatever you have access to. No special tools required.
  • Replace everything in [brackets] with your specifics. The more context you give, the better the output.
  • Treat the output as a first draft, not a final answer. The value is in the structure it gives your thinking, not the exact words.
  • Use the Facilitator prompts when you're leading or planning. Use the Participant prompts when you're contributing or preparing.
  • Chain prompts together: run a stakeholder interview synthesis, then feed the output into a design principles prompt.
  • The best prompt is one you modify. If something doesn't fit your context, edit it. These are starting points, not scripts.
